Detailed Explanation for a Quilter or Fiber Artist:

As quilters and fiber artists know, the finishing touch to any quilt is the binding - the fabric that encases the raw edges to give a quilt a complete look. This journal page illustrates the step-by-step calculation to determine the required length of binding for "Mom's AllSpice Quilt" which measures 81 by 66 and a half inches.

First, the calculations start with the dimensions of the quilt. To calculate the perimeter, you add the length and width (81 inches + 66.5 inches) and then multiply by 2. This gives you the total length of the quilt's edges without the binding. However, binding isn't just a strip sewn around the quilt; it requires extra fabric for mitering the corners and joining the ends. Therefore, a standard additional length - usually about 10 to 12 inches (24 inches in this case, which is quite generous) - is added to the perimeter to ensure there's enough binding.

Once the sum and additional length are combined, the measurement in inches is converted into yards, considering there are 36 inches in a yard. The total comes out to 319 inches, and when divided by 36 inches per yard, the result is approximately 8.86 yards, rounded up to about 9 yards for ease of fabric acquisition.

The note also mentions that they already have 10 yards of fabric, indicating they have more than enough to complete the quilt's binding without the need for purchasing additional materials. As quilters can attest, it's always better to overestimate a tad, allowing for any measurement discrepancies or slight errors in cutting and piecing.
