Detailed Explanation: The journal page in question reflects on the human experience of memory recall and conscious reflection. The text, "I don't get to decide what I remember and what I don't. All I get to decide is what to decide about what I remember," points out a fundamental characteristic of human cognition: the involuntary nature of spontaneous memory recall versus the voluntary control over conscious consideration of memories. The illustration, which appears to depict a pair of eyes, may symbolize the idea of perspective or focus—how we choose to "see" or interpret our memories.

Memory processing involves two key aspects: the encoding/storage phase and the retrieval phase. While encoding and long-term storage of memories can often occur beneath our conscious awareness, shaped by factors such as emotional salience and repetition, retrieval—our ability to access these memories—can be an intentional act. This can involve attentional focus, where one consciously decides which memories to revisit and reflect upon.

However, not all memories can be voluntarily retrieved, a phenomenon that hints at the complicated mechanisms governing memory storage and recall. This includes factors such as decay over time, interference from other memory traces, and the reconstruction of memories during recall, which can lead to alterations each time a memory is accessed.

This journal page insightfully captures a paradox at the heart of memory: we cannot completely control which memories form or when they come to mind, but we can exercise control over the attention we give them and the narrative we construct around our past experiences. This ability to reflect and choose our focus is a powerful aspect of the human psyche and a central theme in various methods of psychotherapy and personal growth strategies.
